What Do Neural Machine Transaltion Model Infers About Multiple Semantics of Homonyms?

Working with the Spoken Language System group at MIT CSAIL, we investigated how well a neural machine translation model infers multiple senses from homonyms. We empirically evaluated the quality of NMT representations for learning the different meanings of homonyms through an extrinsic meaning classification task, by conducting a thorough investigation for both unidirectional and bidirectional NMT models trained with various target languages. We analyzed how various target languages used in training NMT models affect how well NMT representations convey the different meanings of homonyms. This is a necessary first step to incorporate the best meaning disambiguation system to the models, which would improve the NMT model’s ability to translate sentences containing homonyms.

Finding the Bad Apples in the Bunch: Using Robust Optimization to Optimally Structure A Cancer Fund

We utilize robust optimization to optimally structure a cancer megafund that invests in a large number of drug development projects at various stages to substantially reduce the portfolio’s risk. Here, project selection is a crucial aspect in constructing a portfolio which maximizes the return over risk ratio, as we want to allocate capital to high-potential opportunities and avoid the non-performing ones. To determine which projects are worth investing in, we use robust optimization methods to consider the space of all possible subsets of projects, and from those, we identify the worst possible subset, i.e., the bad apples in the bunch.

SCRAM: Securely Measuring Cyber Risk

Working with the Lab of Financial Engineering and Internet Policy Research Initative groups at MIT, we present a solution to cybersecurity challenge by creating a new cryptographic platform that allows multiple firms to compute aggregate measures of cyber risk without requiring any firm to disclose their own sensitive data on cyber attacks, penetrations, and losses.
